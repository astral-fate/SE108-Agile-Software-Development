Key Concepts from Unit 1

Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC) and the Traditional Waterfall Model

The Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC) is a framework describing the phases of software creation (PDF slide 4). The traditional Waterfall Model is a sequential SDLC where each phase must be completed before the next begins (PDF slide 5):

  1. Requirement Gathering
  2. System Analysis
  3. Coding
  4. Testing
  5. Implementation
  6. Operations & Maintenance

This linear flow makes it rigid and difficult to handle changes once a phase is finished (PDF slides 5, 7).

What is Agile Development?

Agile is an umbrella term for software development processes (PDF slide 6) based on (PDF slide 10):

  • Iterative: Development occurs in repeating cycles (iterations). (PDF slide 7)
  • Incremental: Software is built and delivered in functional pieces (increments). (PDF slide 7)
  • Self-organizing: Teams manage their own work to achieve goals, with best designs emerging from them. (PDF slide 10, 13)

It focuses on flexibility, collaboration, and responding to change, unlike rigid traditional methods.

Agile vs. Traditional Methods: Handling Constraints

A fundamental difference lies in how they approach project constraints (Scope/Functionality, Time, Resources) (PDF slide 8):

  • Traditional (Waterfall): Features/Functionality are usually Fixed upfront. Time and Resources are flexible (often leading to delays/overruns to deliver the fixed scope).
  • Agile: Time (iterations) and Resources (team size) are often Fixed. Functionality is flexible and evolves based on priority and feedback, delivering the most valuable features within the fixed constraints.

Agile prioritizes delivering value within predictable timeboxes, while Traditional aims for a fixed scope, often sacrificing time/budget predictability.

The Agile Manifesto: Core Values

The Manifesto defines four core values, stating that while items on the right have value, those on the left are valued more (PDF slide 9):

  • Individuals and interactions over processes and tools
  • Working software over comprehensive documentation (referred to as "Working Product" in PDF slide 9)
  • Customer collaboration over contract negotiation
  • Responding to change over following a plan

This shifts focus to people, functional output, partnership, and adaptability.

Key Agile Principles (Selected)

Supporting the values are 12 principles. Important ones include (PDF slides 11-13):

  • Highest priority is customer satisfaction through early and continuous delivery of valuable software.
  • Welcome changing requirements, even late. Agile processes harness change for customer advantage.
  • Deliver working software frequently (weeks to months, shorter timescale preferred).
  • Business people and developers must work together daily.
  • Build projects around motivated individuals, providing support and trusting them.
  • Most efficient communication is face-to-face conversation.
  • Working software is the primary measure of progress.
  • Promote sustainable development pace.
  • Simplicity—maximizing work not done—is essential.
  • Best designs emerge from self-organizing teams.

Agile Characteristics

Agile development processes typically exhibit the following characteristics (PDF slide 14):

  • Modularity: Process broken into smaller parts.
  • Iterative: Development in short cycles for fast verification/correction.
  • Time-bound: Iteration cycles are fixed (1-6 weeks).
  • Adaptive: Flexible with possible emergent risks.
  • People-oriented: Focus on individuals.
  • Collaborative: Communicative working style.

Advantages of Agile

Key benefits of adopting Agile (PDF slides 15-16):

  • Early Realization of Value: Incremental nature means functional product parts are available early.
  • Visibility & Early Issue Identification: Frequent releases make product visible, helping identify issues sooner.
  • Adaptability to Change: Easier to accept and respond to changes.
  • Increased Customer Satisfaction: Through collaboration and frequent delivery.
  • Producing the Right Product: Iterative process and feedback ensure alignment with needs.

Overview of Agile Methodologies and Frameworks

Agile is an umbrella term. Specific frameworks and methodologies implement Agile principles (PDF slide 17). Examples include:

  • Extreme Programming (XP): Focuses on engineering practices. (PDF slide 18)
  • Scrum: A lightweight framework for managing complexity, using Sprints, roles (Product Owner, Scrum Master, Developer), and events (Daily Meeting). Suitable for small/distributed teams. (PDF slides 36-43)
  • Dynamic Systems Development Method (DSDM)
  • Feature Driven Development (FDD)
  • Crystal Method
  • Adaptive Software Development (ASD)
  • Unified Process (UP)
